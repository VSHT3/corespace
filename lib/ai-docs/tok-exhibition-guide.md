# TOK Exhibition
TOK Exhibition = **internally assessed (IA)** component of 2022 TOK syllabus, replacing old TOK Presentation. Driving question: **"How does TOK manifest itself in the world around us?"** — find real-world evidence of knowledge questions at work, not theoretical essay.

Counts **33% of final TOK grade**. Typically completed **Year 1** of DP.
Student assessment. Student:
1. Selects one of 35 official IB prompts
2. Chooses three real-world objects connecting to prompt
3. Writes justification per object — links object to prompt through knowledge lens

Each object = real, specific — not concept or abstraction. Justifications use TOK concepts (knowledge questions, WOKs, AOKs).
Submit: chosen prompt, images of three objects, commentary. Objects do **not** need to connect to each other — only each independently to prompt.
Worth 33% of TOK grade. Internally assessed, externally moderated.

## What makes a strong exhibition

- Prompt explored through objects, not just described
- Each object genuinely specific and real (particular photograph, named instrument, specific artwork)
- Justification connects object to clear knowledge question
- Three objects show range — different AOKs, contexts, perspectives
- Writing concise and analytical, not descriptive

## Choosing objects

Strong object is
IB interprets broadly:

- Physical item you own (medal, textbook, tool)
- Photograph with specific documented context
- Newspaper article, social media post, political tweet
- Data chart, scientific diagram, map
- Artwork, music score, film still
- Something created *before* exhibition (Extended Essay, art piece)

> ⚠️ **Cannot** create object specifically for exhibition — must already exist with authentic context. Generic stock images don't count. Specificity essential: *your grandfather's 1998 Britannica* stronger than *an encyclopedia*.


## Writing justifications

## What the Commentary Must Do

Per object:
1. **Identify object** + describe specific real-world context
2. **Link explicitly to IA prompt** — use prompt's exact language, don't imply
3. **Justify** why *this specific object* illuminates knowledge question — not describe what it is
4. **Apply TOK concepts** (evidence, perspective, justification, values, certainty) precisely, only where they add meaning
5. **Avoid description** — examiners reward analysis and insight

Objects should represent **different angles** on prompt, not repeat same point three times.

Weak pattern:
"This photograph shows X. X is interesting because it relates to the prompt about Y. This shows that knowledge can be Z."

Strong pattern:
"This object raises the question of [KQ]. It connects to the prompt because [specific analytical link]. This illustrates how [WOK/AOK] shapes what we can know about [topic]."

## Common mistakes

- **Choosing prompt after objects** — start with prompt, find objects that serve it
- **Generic objects** — vague images with no specific context lose marks immediately
- **Description instead of justification** — explaining *what* object is vs. *what it shows about knowledge*
- **Forcing connections** — objects that don't genuinely answer prompt feel contrived
- **Repeating same argument** across all three — aim for variety of angle
- **Jargon dumping** — TOK vocabulary without genuine application hurts
- **Going over 950 words** — limit strict; ~300 words per object

## Linking to TOK Themes

IB strongly recommends grounding exhibition in **Core Theme (Knowledge and the Knower)** or one of school's two **Optional Themes** (e.g., Knowledge and Technology, Knowledge and Language). Contextualizes prompt, strengthens analysis.

## Grading

Out of **10 marks**, single holistic rubric. Marked by TOK teacher, externally moderated by IB.

| Band | Marks | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| Excellent | 9–10 | Insightful; precise, lucid, convincing links to prompt; strong justification; accurate TOK concepts |
| Good | 7–8 | Clear, relevant links; mostly well-reasoned; some genuine insight |
| Satisfactory | 5–6 | Adequate links; uneven; some description rather than analysis |
| Basic | 3–4 | Vague or partial links; mostly descriptive; limited TOK language |
| Rudimentary | 1–2 | Minimal connection to prompt; little justification |
| 0 | 0 | Not submitted, or used non-prescribed prompt |

> Three examiner descriptors for 9–10: **precise, lucid, convincing**.

## Knowledge Questions (KQs) in Justifications

A knowledge question is an open, contestable question about knowledge itself — not about facts of the world.

**Format**: "To what extent does [WOK/AOK] [verb] [knowledge concept]?"

Good examples:
- "To what extent is personal experience a reliable source of knowledge?"
- "How do cultural perspectives shape what we consider evidence?"
- "Can mathematical knowledge exist independently of human minds?"
- "What role does language play in limiting or expanding what we can know?"

Bad (not KQs):
- "Is climate change real?" (empirical question, not about knowledge)
- "Why is art important?" (value question)
- "What is the history of X?" (factual question)

Each object justification should identify at least one KQ that the object raises or exemplifies.

## Ways of Knowing (WOKs)

IB recognises these WOKs (not all must appear in an exhibition):
- **Language** — how words shape and constrain what we can express and know
- **Sense perception** — direct sensory experience as a source of knowledge
- **Emotion** — feelings as evidence or distortion in knowing
- **Reason** — logic and inference as pathways to knowledge
- **Imagination** — the role of creativity and hypothesis in knowing
- **Faith** — knowledge claims based on belief without empirical justification
- **Intuition** — immediate, non-inferential knowing
- **Memory** — reconstructive nature of recalled knowledge

In justifications: don't just *name* a WOK. Explain *how* the specific object reveals something about the WOK's role in knowledge.

## Areas of Knowledge (AOKs)

- Natural Sciences
- Human Sciences
- Arts
- History
- Mathematics
- Ethics
- Indigenous Knowledge Systems
- Religious Knowledge Systems

Three objects ideally span multiple AOKs to show breadth of how the prompt applies across knowledge domains.

## Word Count Guidance

- ~950 words total for all three justifications (IB limit)
- ~300 words per object
- Quality over quantity: examiner stops reading at the limit
