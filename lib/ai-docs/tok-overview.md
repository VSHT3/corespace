# Theory of Knowledge (TOK)

TOK = **sustained conversation about thinking itself** — lens on knowledge, not on subject content.

## The Core Idea

TOK asks one question: **"How do you know?"** Implications enormous. Every belief — history textbook, scientific paper, memory, news headline — produced by *someone*, in *some context*, via *some method*, with *some limitations*. TOK demands you notice and interrogate all of it.

Course rooted in **epistemology** — nature of knowledge — but applied, not abstract. IB wants constant zoom between big conceptual thinking and concrete real-world situations.

## What You Actually Do in TOK

Sit with uncomfortable questions without clean answers:

- Is mathematical proof *more certain* than scientific experiment?
- Can history be objective if historians choose what to record?
- Does word existing in one language but not another mean those cultures *know* different things?
- If powerful institution controls what gets taught — is that *knowledge* or *power*?

Goal: not answers — intellectual discipline to argue *both sides*, acknowledge perspective limits, understand why two intelligent people reach different conclusions from same evidence.

## Why It Matters Beyond the Grade

TOK builds honest thinking. Trains distinction between "I know" and "I strongly believe" — gap most never examine. Fights two failures: **naïve realism** (senses perfectly show reality) and **naïve relativism** (all opinions equally valid). Good TOK lands in uncomfortable middle — knowledge evolves, some claims better justified, certainty always provisional.

Practical: bring TOK into other subjects — question biology methodology, ask whose perspective is absent from history source, ask what it *means* for economic model to be "correct".

## Knowledge questions

Open question about knowledge itself. Good knowledge questions:
- Open-ended (no single correct answer)
- About knowledge, not just topic
- Use TOK language: "to what extent", "how do we know", "what counts as", "what role does X play"

# IB Diploma Programme – Theory of Knowledge (TOK): Complete Context Reference

## What is TOK?

TOK = **compulsory core component** of IB Diploma. Epistemology course — not content subject — central question: **"How do we know what we claim to know?"** Students examine nature, production, justification, and limits of knowledge across disciplines and real-world contexts. Minimum **100 hours classroom instruction** over two-year DP.

One of three DP core components alongside **Extended Essay (EE)** and **CAS (Creativity, Activity, Service)**.

---

## Course Structure (2022 Syllabus)

2022 syllabus replaced "Ways of Knowing" model with **Knowledge Framework**, restructured around one compulsory core theme + school-selected optional themes.

### Core Theme (Compulsory)
**Knowledge and the Knower** — all students. Student's own perspective as knower: biases, epistemic responsibilities, cultural context, intellectual humility.

### Optional Themes (School chooses 2 of 5)
1. Knowledge and Technology
2. Knowledge and Language
3. Knowledge and Politics
4. Knowledge and Religion
5. Knowledge and Indigenous Societies

### Areas of Knowledge (AOKs) — All 5 Compulsory
1. **History** — Knowledge from evidence no longer directly existing; interpretation shapes accounts.
2. **Human Sciences** — Psychology, sociology, economics, anthropology; human behaviour under conditions that complicate objectivity.
3. **Natural Sciences** — Systematic empirical inquiry; observation, experimentation, peer review.
4. **Mathematics** — Formal, deductive knowledge; certainty, abstraction, proof.
5. **The Arts** — Subjective, interpretive knowledge; aesthetics, meaning, expression.

---

## The Knowledge Framework (4 Lenses)

Primary analytical tool in 2022 syllabus. **Four elements** to examine any AOK or Optional Theme:

| Element | Core Question |
|---|---|
| **Scope** | What does this AOK/theme try to explain? What are its boundaries and valid questions? |
| **Perspectives** | Who creates knowledge? Who is excluded? How do culture, bias, and values shape interpretation? |
| **Methods and Tools** | How is knowledge acquired? What evidence, instruments, and methodologies are used? |
| **Ethics** | What are the values, responsibilities, and consequences of producing or applying this knowledge? |

> Note: *Historical Development* sometimes treated as fifth integrated element.

## Assessment

**Two assessed components** — no exam.

### 1. TOK Essay (67% of final TOK grade)
- **Word limit:** Max 1,600 words (references, diagrams, footnotes excluded)
- **Task:** One of six Prescribed Titles issued per IB session (change per May/November session)
- **Requirement:** At least two AOKs; must address Core Theme (Knowledge and the Knower)
- **Marking:** External by IB examiners
- **Rubric:** Holistic, 5-level scale (Rudimentary → Excellent), max **10 marks**; depth over breadth
- **Advice:** Deep two AOKs > shallow many; claims need real-world examples and genuine counterclaims

### 2. TOK Exhibition (33% of final TOK grade)
- **Word limit:** Max **950 words** total (across 3 objects, excluding references)
- **Task:** One of 35 fixed IA prompts (unchanging yearly) + three real-world objects showing TOK in world; objects must be real with specific real-world context
- **Purpose:** Personal engagement with knowledge issues; connects TOK to real world
- **Marking:** Internal (teacher), externally moderated
- **Timing:** Typically **Year 1** of DP

## Grading & Diploma Impact

- TOK graded: **A (Excellent) → E (Elementary)**
- Essay 67%, Exhibition 33% of final TOK grade
- TOK grade combined with EE grade via IB Core Points Matrix → **0–3 bonus diploma points**
- Bonus points added to max 42 subject points → diploma max **45 points**

## Knowledge and the Knower — Core Theme Deep Dive

This is the compulsory core theme. All students study it. Central question: **"How does who I am shape what I know?"**

### Key Questions Under This Theme
- What assumptions do I bring to knowledge claims without realising it?
- How do my cultural background, education, and experiences bias my interpretation of evidence?
- What are my epistemic responsibilities — obligations as someone who produces and shares knowledge?
- What is intellectual humility and why does it matter in knowledge-making?
- How does being inside a community of knowers (discipline, culture, institution) shape what counts as knowledge?

### Knower Perspectives That Affect Knowledge
- **Cognitive biases**: confirmation bias, availability heuristic, anchoring
- **Cultural context**: what counts as legitimate knowledge varies across cultures
- **Positional knowledge**: insider vs outsider perspectives (e.g., understanding poverty)
- **Expert vs layperson**: how credentials affect whose knowledge claims are trusted
- **Shared vs personal knowledge**: individual experience vs collective disciplinary knowledge

### Connecting to Exhibition Objects
When writing justifications, connecting to this core theme means:
- Asking how the person who created/used the object understood knowledge differently from you
- Asking what the object reveals about the limits of the knower's perspective
- Asking what is assumed or taken for granted by the object's creator
- Asking how your position as the knower affects how you read the object
