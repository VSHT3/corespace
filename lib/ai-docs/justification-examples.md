# TOK Exhibition Justification Examples and Phrase Banks

## What an excellent justification looks like

Each justification (95–150 words) must:
1. Open with or embed a specific Knowledge Question (KQ)
2. Identify the object specifically (not generically)
3. Link the object's specific properties to the IA prompt — not to a topic, but to a knowledge question the object raises
4. Name at least one WOK or AOK and show (not just state) how it operates through this object
5. Close with an insight: what does this object reveal about how we know, what we believe, or how knowledge works?

## Annotated example — strong

**Prompt 12:** "Can there be knowledge that is independent of culture?"
**Object:** A Japanese shōgi board manufactured in 1954, owned by the student's grandfather

> "Does the structure of a strategy game encode cultural knowledge, or does it express universal human reasoning? My grandfather's shōgi board—purchased in Osaka in 1954—suggests this question has no clean answer. The board's spatial logic (9×9 grid, piece promotion, hidden piece faces) differs fundamentally from chess, yet both games train what IB calls 'reason' as a WOK. This implies that reasoning strategies—typically assumed to be culture-independent—are partly shaped by the cultural artefact through which they are practised. The shōgi board therefore challenges the assumption that mathematical or logical knowledge is purely abstract: it shows that even formal reasoning develops within cultural containers. Culture does not just provide context; it participates in what we can know and how we come to know it."

**Why this works:**
- Opens with a real KQ framed around the specific object
- Object is named specifically (1954, Osaka, grandfather's)
- Connects object properties (board structure) to WOK (reason) analytically
- Generates insight (cultural containers shape even "pure" reasoning), not just observation
- No jargon dumped — WOK named only once and applied

## Annotated example — weak (common mistakes shown)

**Prompt 12:** "Can there be knowledge that is independent of culture?"
**Object:** "A chess board"

> "A chess board is a game that has been played around the world for centuries. It is found in many cultures, which shows that knowledge can transcend cultural boundaries. Chess involves logic and reasoning, which are universal ways of knowing. This relates to the prompt because it shows how knowledge can be independent of culture since chess is played everywhere. In conclusion, this object demonstrates that some knowledge is indeed culture-independent."

**Why this fails:**
- Object is generic ("a chess board" — no specificity, no authenticity)
- Opens with description, not KQ
- "Shows that knowledge can transcend cultural boundaries" — conclusion stated without analytical support
- WOK (reasoning) named but not analysed
- "In conclusion" — formulaic, no insight added
- Circular reasoning: the claim and the evidence are the same statement

## Annotated example — strong (different prompt)

**Prompt 6:** "How does the way we organize or classify things shape what we know about them?"
**Object:** The DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th edition), a specific copy from a therapist's office

> "What does placing human behaviour inside categorical boundaries do to our knowledge of the mind? The DSM-5 on my therapist's bookshelf classifies 157 distinct mental disorders—each a named boundary around what had previously been a spectrum of experience. The manual's existence raises a structural question: does the act of classification create knowledge, or merely organize prior uncertainty into the illusion of certainty? From the perspective of the Human Sciences AOK, the DSM's categories are both diagnostic tools and normative claims: naming 'depression' as a distinct entity shapes what clinicians look for, what patients expect to feel, and what constitutes 'normal.' The object reveals how classification is never neutral—it participates in producing the very reality it claims to describe."

**Why this works:**
- Opens with KQ directly tied to object properties
- Object specific: named, numbered edition, personal context
- Connects classification mechanics to AOK (Human Sciences) analytically
- Shows feedback loop (classification shapes what is known)
- No unnecessary terminology

## Phrase bank: opening Knowledge Questions

Use these as templates — fill the brackets:

- "What does it mean to [claim X] about [domain], and who gets to decide?"
- "Does [WOK] help us understand [phenomenon], or does it constrain what we can see?"
- "When [object type] is used to [function], what assumptions about knowledge does it encode?"
- "To what extent is [knowledge claim] a matter of [WOK1] rather than [WOK2]?"
- "If [different culture/perspective] encountered this object, would they draw the same knowledge from it?"
- "Can knowledge produced through [WOK] be considered objective, or is it always embedded in [context]?"

---

## Phrase bank: analytical connectors (avoid overuse)

- "The specific provenance of this object matters because…"
- "This is not merely descriptive — it reveals that…"
- "What distinguishes this object from a generic example is…"
- "The prompt's question becomes concrete here: [object property] shows that…"
- "This challenges the assumption that [common belief about knowledge]…"
- "Rather than simply illustrating [WOK/AOK], the object unsettles it by…"

---

## Red flags — phrases that weaken justifications

Avoid:
- "This relates to the prompt because…" (state the relationship, don't announce it)
- "This shows that knowledge is important / complex / subjective" (truism)
- "In conclusion" (no conclusions needed — maintain analytical voice throughout)
- "This is an example of [WOK]" (name + analyse, don't just label)
- "As TOK teaches us…" (don't reference the course)
- "This object is interesting because…" (tell us *why* it's analytically significant)
- "Everyone knows that…" / "It is obvious that…" (avoid appeals to common knowledge)

---

## Justification structure (template)

1. **KQ sentence** (1 sentence) — open question about knowledge that this object raises
2. **Object identification** (1 sentence) — specific name, context, date, provenance
3. **Object-to-prompt link** (2–3 sentences) — what specific property of the object connects to the prompt question; use prompt's exact words
4. **WOK/AOK analysis** (2–3 sentences) — how a specific Way of Knowing or Area of Knowledge operates through or is illuminated by this object
5. **Insight** (1–2 sentences) — what this reveals about knowledge that you couldn't say without this specific object
