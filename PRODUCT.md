# Product

## Register

product

## Users

The primary users are individual IB Diploma students working independently on Core requirements, especially Theory of Knowledge. They are usually trying to turn a vague IB task into a concrete next step: choose a prompt, understand what it asks, shape object choices, draft justifications, and get feedback before teacher review.

Teachers and coordinators may use or inspect the product occasionally, but the interface should be optimized first for student self-service rather than classroom administration.

## Product Purpose

Corespace helps IB students make steady progress through TOK, CAS, and Extended Essay work with structured workflows and AI-assisted feedback. The current product focus is the TOK Exhibition helper: students pick an official prompt, build an exhibition around three objects, and improve their justifications with targeted feedback.

Success means a student can understand what to do next without needing a generic chatbot prompt, a long rubric reread, or a teacher intervention for every small decision.

## Brand Personality

Focused, feedback-providing, candid, and student-native. The product should feel like student-to-student help from someone who understands the IB workload: direct, practical, and specific, without pretending to be an official authority.

The tone should be clear and constructive. It should explain tradeoffs, point out weak reasoning, and give usable next steps without sounding corporate, childish, or falsely motivational.

## Anti-references

Corespace should not feel like a generic AI wrapper, a corporate edtech dashboard, a childish school app, or an over-polished SaaS marketing site.

Avoid vague AI assistant framing, decorative productivity theater, enterprise admin density, mascot-like friendliness, and marketing polish that makes the tool feel less trustworthy for actual student work.

## Design Principles

1. Make the next academic move obvious. Every screen should help the student decide what to do next, not just show available features.
2. Feedback should be specific enough to act on. Generic encouragement is less useful than a clear critique, example, or rewrite direction.
3. Keep the student in control. AI should support thinking and drafting, not imply that generated text is submission-ready.
4. Respect IB seriousness without becoming institutional. The product should feel credible for real coursework while staying student-native.
5. Prefer structured workflows over open-ended chat. Students should not need to invent the process before getting help.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA as the baseline. Preserve keyboard access for all controls, visible focus states, readable contrast, and predictable layouts on mobile and desktop.

Use reduced-motion-friendly interactions where animation exists, especially in the TOK prompt tour. Avoid relying on color alone for meaning, since the TOK interface uses category colors heavily.
